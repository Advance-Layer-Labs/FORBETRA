## Section 1 — The One-Sentence Truth

Forbetra is the first coaching platform where the people around you become part of your growth — not once a year, but every week — and where artificial intelligence turns the patterns in that feedback into a coaching conversation that begins before the coach has even said hello.

(That is the lede. Every other sentence in this document supports it.)

## Section 2 — The Problem, in Human Terms

Most professional growth happens in a vacuum.

You set a goal in January. You meet your coach once a month. In between, you forget what you committed to within four days. Your manager has opinions about how you're doing but never tells you in time to do anything about them. Your peers see things you can't see — the tone you take in meetings, the moments you avoid, the wins you undersell — and they say nothing, because no one ever asks. By the time the formal "360 review" comes around twelve months later, the data is so old it might as well be a memoir.

Coaching today runs on conversations and gut feel. Coaches walk into sessions and spend the first fifteen minutes catching up. Clients show up unsure of what changed. The most valuable perspective in the room — how other people experience you — isn't in the room at all.

This isn't a tooling problem. It's a feedback-loop problem. And feedback loops, when they're broken, don't close themselves.

**Forbetra closes the loop.**

## Section 3 — The Name

Forbetra is the Icelandic word for "to improve." It sits in the imperative — a quiet command. Not "improvement" as a noun, not "improving" as a state, but _forbetra_ as a verb you do. The name is short, foreign enough to be memorable, and grounded enough not to feel marketed. The brand voice borrows from this: declarative, unornamented, practical. Like Scandinavian design furniture that does one thing perfectly and doesn't apologize for the things it doesn't do.

(Pronunciation note for narration: "for-BEH-tra," with stress on the second syllable.)

## Section 4 — The Four People in the Loop

Every Forbetra story has four characters. The video should personify each one.

### The Individual

Meet **Maya Chen**, a forty-two-year-old VP of Product at a mid-stage SaaS company. Her boss told her she needs to be "more strategic." She doesn't know what that means in practice. She's been working with an executive coach for three months and the conversations are good — but by Wednesday she's forgotten what they talked about on Monday. She wants accountability that fits between meetings, not another tab to check.

In Forbetra, Maya does one thing each week: she rates her own effort and her own performance against her current development objective. It takes her sixty seconds. The numbers themselves matter less than the act — she's been forced to pause, look back at her week, and decide what to call it.

### The Stakeholder

Meet **James Okafor**, a senior engineer who reports to Maya. He's been wanting to tell her she interrupts him in meetings, but it has never been the right moment. Twice a week he gets a short email from Forbetra. He clicks. He doesn't have to log in. He rates Maya's effort and performance from where he sits, optionally writes a single observation, and he's done in ninety seconds.

He doesn't see Maya's self-ratings while he's submitting his own. After he submits, the platform reveals the comparison: he rated her performance a six; she rated herself an eight. He sees that gap. So does Maya. So does her coach.

### The Coach

Meet **Dr. Elena Vasquez**, an ICF Master Certified Coach with eight executive clients. Every Monday morning, she opens Forbetra and sees a generated briefing for each of them: how Maya rated herself last week, what her stakeholders said, where the gap is widening, what specific behavior to ask about. The briefing took thirty seconds to generate. It would have taken Elena two hours to assemble manually.

By the time Maya walks into Tuesday's session, Elena already knows the question to start with.

### The AI

The fourth character has no name. It is Anthropic's Claude, running in the background, reading the data nobody else has time to read, finding the patterns nobody else has time to find, writing the synthesis nobody else has time to write. It does not replace the coach. It clears the runway so the coach can land.

## Section 5 — The Weekly Rhythm

The product runs on a rhythm. The video should feel like a week passing in time-lapse.

### Monday morning, 9:00 AM

Maya gets a notification: _Your check-in is ready._ She opens Forbetra on her phone in the back of an Uber. Two screens, three taps, one optional sentence about what happened last week. Submitted by 9:02.

### Tuesday afternoon, 3:00 PM

Three of Maya's stakeholders — her boss, James, and a peer — get their feedback request. Each clicks the link, rates her on the same two dimensions, optionally adds a behavioral observation, sees the post-submission reveal that compares their rating to Maya's. Done by 3:04.

### Sunday night, 8:00 PM

A cron job fires. The AI synthesizes the week. It looks at the trend across Maya's last six self-ratings. It compares them to the stakeholder average. It identifies the perception gap (currently 1.7 points on performance — Maya rates herself higher than others do). It writes a behavioral experiment: "In your next leadership meeting, ask one clarifying question before sharing your view, and notice whether the conversation shifts."

### Monday morning, 7:00 AM

Elena opens her coach dashboard. Maya's prep card is ready. Stability score, trajectory, perception gap, suggested topics, recent stakeholder observations, Elena's own previous notes — all on one screen. Elena reads it in two minutes. Adds a note. Walks into the 9:00 AM session with her opening question already drafted.

### The Loop Closes

Maya tries the experiment. Her stakeholders notice. They rate her differently the next week. The gap narrows. The AI sees the gap narrow. The coach sees the gap narrow. Maya sees the gap narrow. Everyone in the loop is reinforced for staying in the loop. The flywheel turns.

### Scene 3 — The Check-in

**Capture:** `/individual/checkin` — record the entire interaction in real time.
**State:** Cycle is active, current week unfilled, previous week's ratings visible.
**Narration:** "The check-in is the heartbeat. Two scores, zero to ten — effort and performance. An optional note. Sixty seconds, on phone or laptop. **Capture:** Same flow as Scene 3, but on a check-in that triggers a milestone (week 7, 14, 21).
**Narration:** "Forbetra does not over-celebrate. There are no confetti animations, no balloon GIFs. **Graphic:** Highlight the streak badge subtly — slow zoom, then fade.

### Scene 5 — The Insight

**Capture:** `/individual/insights` — click "Generate Report" and let the AI streaming response render in real time. **Do not edit the streaming.** That is the magic.
**Narration:** "Now watch this. The AI reads twelve weeks of data — the individual's self-ratings, the stakeholder feedback, the behavioral observations — and writes the insight live, on screen. Not a report you wait for in your inbox. A document that materializes in front of you, section by section, in the time it takes to pour a cup of coffee."
**Graphic:** Subtle pulse animation in the corner indicating "live generation." A counter showing tokens generated per second is too technical — instead, a soft color shift on each new section as it lands.

### Scene 6 — The Perception Gap

**Capture:** Scroll down inside the generated insight to the perception-gap section, OR navigate to `/individual/scorecard` for the dedicated view.
**State:** A real gap should exist — choose a seed account where self ≠ stakeholder by at least 1.5 points.

## Section 7 — Three Things That Make It Different (Use This for the Cold-Open or Mid-Roll Sizzle)

1. **Continuous, not episodic.** Traditional 360 reviews happen once a year. Forbetra collects stakeholder feedback every week. The perception gap closes in real time, not in retrospect.

2. **AI as interpreter, not just collector.** Most coaching platforms show you charts and let you draw your own conclusions. Forbetra reads the patterns, names them, and tells you what to try next. The data is interpreted, not just displayed.

3. **Built for sixty seconds.** Every interaction — the check-in, the stakeholder feedback, the insight review — is engineered to fit between meetings. No training required. No workshops. A busy executive uses it the way they use a thermostat.

## Section 11 — Voice and Tone Direction for Narration

- **Pace:** Measured. This is not a hype reel. The product is quietly confident; the narration should match.
- **Diction:** Plain English. Avoid jargon ("synergy," "stakeholder ecosystem," "behavioral analytics platform" — banned). Use names ("Maya," "Elena," "James") whenever possible.
- **Sentence length:** Mix short and long. Short sentences for emphasis. Longer sentences for the texture of the user's lived experience.
- **Emotional register:** Calm authority. The product is a relief, not a thrill. It removes friction from a frustrating process.
- **What to avoid:** Words like "revolutionary," "game-changing," "disruptive." Forbetra is not those things. It is something more useful — _consistent_. The narration should sound like it was written by someone who has actually used the product, not someone who is selling it.

**Reference voices:** The narration should feel closer to Apple's product-launch voiceovers (Jony Ive era) than to a SaaS demo. Or to the Headspace app's morning meditations — quiet, deliberate, present.

## Section 12 — Music and Sound Design Direction

- **Music:** Minimalist piano or restrained electronic ambient. Suggested artists for licensing reference: Nils Frahm, Ólafur Arnalds, Tycho (slow tracks). Avoid "corporate uplifting acoustic" stock music at all costs.
- **Sound design:** Soft, occasional UI sounds layered under the screen captures (a quiet click, a gentle whoosh on transitions). Nothing percussive.
- **Silence:** Use silence intentionally. After the perception-gap reveal in Scene 6, hold for two beats with no music. After the line _"This is what you cannot see alone,"_ hold for three.

## Section 13 — Visual Style Direction for Motion Graphics

- **Color palette:** Match the product. Primary text in soft black on warm off-white. Accent in the product's blue. Use the product's actual gradient (visible in the onboarding flow) as the only ornamental gradient in the whole video.
- **Typography:** Match the product's font (Inter or system stack). No display fonts. No serifs.
- **Animation style:** Restrained. Slow fade-ins, gentle scale transitions. No bounce, no spring physics, no "dynamic kinetic typography" trends. The product is dignified; the motion should be too.
- **Overlay style:** When highlighting a UI element, use a soft white halo at low opacity, not a hard ring or arrow. When labeling, use minimal text in the same Inter font, positioned with margin, not crammed onto the UI.
- **Transitions:** Cross-dissolves between scenes. No hard cuts inside a single product flow. Hard cuts are reserved for transitions between user types (Individual → Stakeholder → Coach).
